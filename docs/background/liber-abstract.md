TRUST framework for evaluating AI-based information search tools

S. Mok, S.A. Kruit, G. de Jonge, K.I.H. Mingoti Poague, L. van Ewijk, J.M. van Eck
Affiliation: Embedded Information Services,
Library, ICT Services & Archive,
University of Twente,
Enschede, Netherlands

Key words: Trustworthy AI, information search tools, academic integrity

Novel information search tools are changing how academics access knowledge. These AI-driven systems should augment, not replace, users’ critical thinking and information-literacy skills. As information specialists, our role is to act as informed intermediaries, guiding the scientific community toward tools that enhance research without compromising academic integrity [1]. Several principles and guidelines for designing and using trustworthy AI have been developed [2] alongside metrics for assessing these principles [3]. However, no current specific, rigorous methods to evaluate AI-based search tools exist. This abstract introduces the TRUST framework, a practical tool designed to evaluate AI-based search tools. It measures whether recommended tools are reliable, ethical, and effective, and match scholarly and governmental standards of rigor and transparency.

The TRUST framework is built on five core principles, inspired by the FAIR principles for data management [4]. Each tool review will grade the subject on these aspects:
•	Transparent: Methodology, data sources, and limitations are clearly documented.
•	Reliable: Outputs are accurate, verifiable, and consistent. Sources exist and are not hallucinated.
•	User-centric: Fit for academic use and integrates with standard research workflows.
•	Secure: Protects user privacy and complies with GDPR and institutional/national/ international policies.
•	Traceable: All information generated and/or used can be traced back to a primary source.

Literature stresses that AI models operate as "black boxes," making documentation alone insufficient for deep trust and transparency [5]. The TRUST evaluation protocol addresses challenges such as “consistent and reproducible results” from AI-based literature searches. However, this remains a critical point of failure in the current AI landscape due to the methods of retrieval and synthesis of information AI tools use [6]. Similarly, the framework checks if output is “factually accurate and verifiable” and “faithful to source material”, which especially generic large language models (LLMs) consistently fail to provide [7]. These evaluation protocol items are the main reason general LLMs are mostly categorized as “not recommended” for AI-based literature search. In order for these tools to pass these checks, they must evolve beyond keyword retrieval toward a nuanced understanding of academic context and hierarchy [8].

Our team of information specialists piloted the TRUST evaluation protocol. Examples of results include:
•	Recommended: ai2 Asta - Restricts outputs to the Semantic Scholar corpus, exposes reasoning chain, and is free and mostly open-sourced. It excels in Traceability and Reliability.
•	Needs review/provisional: Elicit - Valuable for abstract summarization, but its ranking algorithm is not fully disclosed, limiting its Transparency. The free version is capped, affecting its User-centric score.
•	Not recommended: generic LLM Interfaces (e.g., ChatGPT, Gemini) and general search-focused AI (e.g., Perplexity) - Prone to citation errors and lack rigorous, verifiable academic sources, failing the Reliability and Traceability criteria.

In the coming months, the TRUST framework will be refined, establishing a standardized evaluation protocol and interactive integrated workflow. This proposed workflow will use structured online forms for data entry coupled with automatic processing, ending with a database that can be used to eventually serve a public-facing list of approved tools accompanied by user guides. In the longer term, TRUST could be extended beyond search tools, to also assess AI features embedded in existing platforms, and integrate into institutional AI-policies and initiatives.
