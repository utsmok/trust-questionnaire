TRUST framework addendums from literature
The TRUST framework, while aimed to be well-aligned with key ethical and technical concerns in the literature, contains several gaps where recent research identifies critical mechanisms or persistent empirical challenges not fully captured by the high-level principles or checklist items.
Below you can find the identified gaps in the TRUST framework's operational checklist, contrasted with the findings in academic sources:
Gaps in checklist
1. Lack of explicit requirement for explainable AI (XAI) mechanisms (gap in T - Transparent)
While the Transparent principle (T) mandates that methodology and limitations must be clearly documented, the checklist lacks explicit requirements for how the AI's processes should be exposed, an issue central to XAI research.
Lacking mechanism: The literature stresses that AI models operate as "black boxes," making documentation alone insufficient for deep trust,. Tools like Valsci address this by using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting to structurally synthesize evidence and generate a transparent, step-by-step reasoning process,. This mechanism ensures that the decision-making process is transparent and auditable.
Recommendation: While T5 (Traceable) addresses whether steps are "auditable", the checklist under T2. Methodology could be enhanced to require disclosure of RAG components (e.g., the specific LLM model used, the number of top documents fed for summarization, and whether CoT reasoning is implemented),,.
2. Failure to mandate robust usability and human agency evaluation (gap in U - User-Centric)
The User-centric principle (U) requires the tool to be "fit for academic use" and "Useable for intended audience" (U3), but it omits specific, established criteria for evaluating user interface design and cognitive risks.
Lacking standardized metrics: The framework does not explicitly incorporate established usability evaluation heuristics (such as Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics) which remain durable and relevant for assessing modern interfaces.
Lacking cognitive guardrails: Research emphasizes that AI-assisted tools may increase automation bias and hinder critical thinking. The framework sets the philosophical goal of augmenting critical thinking, but the U-checklist points (U1-U3) do not explicitly require the tool design to mitigate automation bias, ensure user agency and control, or provide effective support for critical evaluation. Furthermore, effective tool utilization is predicated on Generative AI Literacy (GLAT), which is a high-level educational objective,, but is not assessed as a functional metric of the tool itself under U.
3. Underrepresentation of algorithmic bias and fairness as a core compliance criterion (gap in S - Secure)
The Secure principle (S) correctly focuses on regulatory compliance (GDPR, EU AI Act), but the checklist largely treats bias as a generalized limitation (T3) rather than a critical compliance and ethical risk (S).
Bias as an ethical mandate: Literature insists that libraries must critically engage with "data bias" and "algorithmic bias," recognizing that collections are inherently discriminatory and AI implementations can amplify these prejudices,.
Recommendation: While S3 covers general safety guidelines, the checklist should be strengthened to include specific evaluation of algorithmic fairness and how potential biases in the training data (a limitation often overlooked in LLMs) or output (perpetuating biases) are detected and mitigated.
4. Need for explicit bibliometric credibility scoring (refinement for T - Traceable)
The Traceable principle (T) is focused on ensuring links to primary sources (T4) and auditing the process (T5),. However, modern scientific claim verification tools incorporate measures beyond mere retrieval to assess source credibility.
Lacking credibility metrics: An advanced verification system like Valsci integrates a structured bibliometric scoring mechanism that ranks retrieved papers based on criteria such as the author's H-index, citation counts, and estimated journal impact,. This scoring offers a nuanced view of source credibility that moves beyond simply confirming the citation exists (T4) and helps weigh the evidence.
Recommendation: Adding a criterion under Traceable or Reliable that evaluates whether the system provides mechanisms for source credibility scoring (e.g., integrating citation metrics or impact factors) would reflect best practices in advanced academic search evaluation.
Aspects challenged by literature
Reliability (R2): consistency and reproducibility
The TRUST framework explicitly requires that outputs are "Consistent and reproducible results" (R2). However, extensive empirical studies focusing on AI academic search engines strongly suggest this requirement is highly aspirational and often unsupported by the current generation of tools.
Empirical contradiction: Several studies found that AI tools frequently demonstrate a lack of repeatability and reliability even when given identical queries, with results varying significantly across multiple trials,. For example, tests on Elicit showed inconsistent results across three trials when attempting to identify relevant studies for a systematic review,.
Technical challenge: This inconsistency stems from non-deterministic search strategies used by Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems, where Large Language Models (LLMs) may be used to generate Boolean search strategies or assist in query expansion, leading to variable search results,.
Implication: For TRUST to remain rigorous, it must acknowledge that R2 is a critical point of failure for many AI tools and requires robust methodological checks to quantify variation, rather than assuming reproducibility is achievable in the current AI landscape.
Reliability (R1/R3): Accuracy and Faithfulness
The framework requires outputs to be "Factually accurate and verifiable" (R1) and "faithful to source material" (R3). The literature overwhelmingly supports this need but also shows that AI systems systematically fail this test, especially regarding precision.
Empirical failure: Comparative studies found that AI platforms like Elicit yielded the most results but had an extremely high false positive rate (94%). General LLMs consistently produce hallucinations or fabricated references, failing in accuracy and verifiability,,.
Implication: This highlights the necessity of human oversight, as mandated by the policy's vision, but reinforces that R1 and R3 are the most significant reasons why general LLMs are categorized as "Not recommended". The literature suggests that for R1/R3 to be met, AI tools must evolve beyond keyword retrieval toward a nuanced understanding of academic context and hierarchy,.
